Methodology & Assumptions

How we estimate whether solar makes sense for your home.

This page explains, in plain language, how our estimates are created and what they’re based on. If you want technical detail, it’s here—but nothing is hidden behind jargon.

Methodology Version: 1.2
Last Updated: January 2026
Geographic Calibration: Santa Cruz County, CA

The big picture (read this first

Every report follows the same steps:

  1. We look at your roof
  2. We account for local sunlight and weather
  3. We estimate a realistic system size
  4. We compare expected production to local costs and utility rates
  5. We give a clear recommendation

No sales adjustment. No best-case assumptions presented as truth.

What information we use

Each assessment combines a small number of real-world inputs:

  • your address: To understand roof direction, local sunlight, and utility rates
  • Roof photos: To estimate slope, shading, and usable space for panels
  • Loacl sunlight data: From the U.S. National Renewable Energy Laboratory (NREL), based on historical weather patterns
  • Utility rates: Current PG&E electricity rates and net metering policies for Santa Cruz County

That’s it. We don’t use personal data or installer quotes.

What you recieve

Each report includes:

  • Estimated annual energy production: How much electricity a typical system on your roof could produce
  • Estimated savings range: A realistic range showing potential bill reduction over time
  • Payback estimate: How long it may take for savings to offset installation costs
  • Environmental impact: Estimated reduction in carbon emissions
  • A clear recommendation: Whether solar appears financially sensible for your home—and why

Our standard assumptions (kept conservative)

When property-specific details aren’t available, we use widely accepted, conservative defaults:

Parameter Assumption
System size Based on usable roof space (not full coverage)
Panel efficiency Modern solar panel efficiency: 19-21% (current standard panels)
System losses Typical system losses from wiring, inverter, and temperature: 14% total
Annual degradation Gradual performance decline over time: 0.5% per year
System lifespan Standard residential system lifespan: 25 years
Installation cost Recent local installation cost averages: $2.80-$3.20 per watt before incentives

These assumptions are designed to reflect typical outcomes, not optimistic scenarios.

Analysis process

  1. Roof assessment: Computer vision analysis extracts pitch angle and identifies shading obstacles from submitted photographs
  2. Orientation determination: Address geocoding determines roof azimuth and confirms findings from image analysis
  3. Production modeling: Data inputs feed into NREL PVWatts model adjusted for property-specific factors
  4. Economic calculation: Production estimates combined with utility rates and installation costs to project savings
  5. Feasibility assessment: Results evaluated against minimum viability thresholds (payback period, production levels, cost-benefit ratio)

What could affect accuracy

Estimates may differ from actual outcomes due to:

  • Roof structural factors not visible in photographs (age, need for reinforcement)
  • Local permitting costs or requirements beyond typical ranges
  • Actual electricity usage patterns differing from assumptions
  • Changes in utility rate structures or net metering policies
  • Installation cost variations based on specific contractor and complexity
  • Weather patterns deviating from historical averages
  • Equipment performance varying from manufacturer specifications

Sources and standards

Production modeling based on:

  • NREL PVWatts Calculator (version 8)
  • PG&E published rate schedules and net metering tariffs

Cost data derived from:

  • California Solar & Storage Association installer surveys
  • EnergySage marketplace data for Santa Cruz County
